ShippingHub Co-op Management Corporation


The ShippingHub Co-op Management Corporation is the central organization tool which will permit farmers of all sizes to collaborate on marketing, pricing and shipping, maximizing profit while minimizing both cost and spoilage.

Through the implementation of the Co-op, each participating farmer will receive a Natural Area Code (NAC). The code represents their particular address and serves as a mechanism under which any data that is static, or consistent with every shipment, may be stored. The NAC also identifies the type of goods the farmer produces and facilitates the ability to track and trace goods back to their point of origin. The Co-op will issue labels to farmers with these codes, eliminating the need for them to have a computer or access to the internet. New data, such as weight or quantity of the goods, will be entered under the NAC by representatives of the Co-op at the first link in the supply chain with the technological capacity to do so.

ShippingHub Co-op will also organize consolidation points for goods and arrange for their movement to the ShippingHub Super DC. For agricultural produce, the Co-op will implement cold-chain storage through the distribution of ShippingHub Pods and Trailers, preserving and extending the life of crops for export.

Why is the ability to track and trace product shipments important?

In the near future, specialty organic farmers in Jamaica will be able to ship fresh produce directly to Groceries and Restaurants in Canada and USA. Demand for organic produce is on the rise, with consumers more interested in knowing where their food comes from. The NAC will pinpoint the origin of crops and keeping farming data current will make it easier to award organic certifications.

Excerpt from “A tracking system for organic farmers”, by Tracy Lundberg-Schimpf, Kelowna, B.C., posted on Cyber-Help for Organic Farmers.

Traceability is the new buzz word in agriculture. Mad cows, botulism and bioterrorism have created a huge demand for traceability in our food system. The purpose of a tracking system for farmers is not much different than that of similar systems used by manufacturing companies. It enables the farmer to trace their final product from the customer back to the field and materials it came from and pinpoint any possible contamination. The same process is necessary in any type of manufacturing environment in any industry. It gives customers confidence that, should anything go wrong, the entire history of that product is readily available.

Even though most farmers know where their products came from and what was done to them, the current expectation is that the farmer can prove that knowledge with some type of documentation. A paper trail has always been a requirement of organic certification.
