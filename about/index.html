Vision & Mission

Vision

Our vision is of a world in which trade is democratized; where all countries have both equal access to global markets and an equal capacity to leverage that access to grow their economies, feed their peoples, and end poverty. It is with this second aspect – leveraging access – that we believe we can help.

Mission

Currently, many developing low and middle income countries have legal access to global markets either through free-trade agreements or through some form of favoured nation status with wealthier nations.

However, legal access is not enough.

Developing countries and their economies often lack the resources to leverage this legal access. They lack the efficient communication and transport infrastructure to keep costs down while domestic businesses – usually micro-, small-, or medium-sized enterprises (MSMEs) – individually lack the size or scale necessary to realize the cost advantages of the economies of scale available to the developed world’s large companies and economies. With the cost barrier in place, few of these MSMEs ship goods out. The lack of frequency of exports drives up the costs of shipping goods both into and out of the country. The high cost barrier is reinforced, and it becomes a self-defeating loop.

Improving the internal communication and transportation logistics of a developing country will lead to rapid advancements in its economic growth, its ability to deliver public services, and its ability to take advantage of global trade for the benefit of its people.

Our mission is to deliver the ShippingHub National Logistics Strategy (SNLS) with the goal of assisting low and middle income countries in leveraging their access to global markets. The Strategy consists of a suite of technology and organization solutions designed to spur economic development and to reduce hunger and poverty. By implementing logistic solutions that will boost efficiencies associated with the movement of goods, we will lower the cost barriers currently blocking access to these markets.
